Why Hummingbirds are important?

Hummingbirds play an important role in the food web, pollinating a variety of fl owering plants, some of which are specifi cally adapted to pollination by hummingbirds. Some tropical hummingbirds are at risk, like other pollinators, due to habitat loss and changes in the distribution and abundance of nectar plants.

Life cycle

Hummingbirds belong to a group of animals called oviparous. They contribute to the cycle of life by laying eggs

Eggs

Hummingbird eggs are tiny and oval-shaped, approximately the size of a small pea. They range in size from 0.5 to 0.8 inches (1.3-2 cm) long and weigh approximately 0.4 grams. The eggs have a hard, smooth shell and are often white or white with a bluish or greenish tint. Most females lay two eggs, which they incubate for 15 to 18 days.

Embryo

Hummingbirds, like all bird species, begin life as embryos in a hard-shelled egg in a nest. The mother bird will keep the eggs warm, or incubate them, for 10-15 days until they hatch.

Hatchlings

When they hatch, baby hummingbirds or hatchlings are very small. They weigh less than a gram (lighter than a single paperclip) and they're HUNGRY. The mother hummingbird spends a lot of her time catching bugs to feed her babies during this phase.

Adult

Hummingbirds usually learn to fly by the time they're 21 days old. Many hummingbirds don’t survive their first year of life, usually because they don’t eat enough, they are hunted, or they get injured. Those that do survive can live 3-5 years.

Structure

Like all birds, the body of a hummingbirds is divided into three main sections: bipedal and have wings and feathers.

Hummingbirds species

There are over 350 known hummingbird species, with new species being described every year. There also used to be another two species of hummingbird that have gone extinct since they were first discovered in the 19th century. Hummingbirds are their own family, known as Trochilidae.

Habitat

Hummingbirds are the ultimate backyard birds, but they thrive in a wide variety of habitats beyond the typical landscaped suburban backyard.

These are diverse, adaptable birds that can be found in a wide range of climatic and ecological zones, and understanding hummingbird habitats can help birders know where to look for these flying jewels in the field as well as plan a better backyard oasis to attract hummingbirds at home.

Migration

It is believed that the first hummingbirds developed in South America after arriving from Asia 22 million years ago. Once they spread through South America, a few species began to move to Central America, the Caribbean and eventually mainland North America. By migrating to areas with more abundant food, these little explorers had less competition for food and territory. Of course, the seasonal cooling also drove these species south every fall. This cycle of advancing and retreating with the seasons is the basic foundation of their migratory pattern.

Tigger

Although there are differing views in the birding community as to what triggers the start of migration, it is generally thought that hummingbirds sense changes in daylight duration, and changes in the abundance of flowers, nectar and insects. Instinct also plays a role in making the decision to migrate.

Making the Trip

During migration, a hummingbird's heart beats up to 1,260 times a minute, and its wings flap 15 to 80 times a second. To support this high energy level, a hummingbird will typically gain 25-40% of their body weight before they start migration in order to make the long trek over land, and water.

They fly alone, often on the same path they have flown earlier in their life, and fly low, just above tree tops or water. Young hummingbirds must navigate without parental guidance.

Hummingbirds fly by day when nectar sources such as flowers are more abundant. Flying low allows the birds to see, and stop at, food supplies along the way. They are also experts at using tail winds to help reach their destination faster and by consuming less energy and body fat. Research indicates a hummingbird can travel as much as 23 miles in one day. However those that make the 500 mile flight from Florida to the Yucatan do it in 18-22 hours non-stop, depending on wind conditions.

Hummingbirds populations

Hummingbirds are facing a number of threats, including habitat loss, climate change, pesticides, predators, and disease. We can help protect hummingbirds by planting native flowers, creating hummingbird feeders, supporting conservation organizations, and educating others about the threats facing hummingbirds.

Ways to help

Plant native flowersHummingbirds need nectar from flowers to survive. By planting native flowers in our gardens, we can provide them with a reliable source of food. Native flowers are also more likely to attract hummingbirds than non-native flowers.

Create hummingbird feeders: Hummingbird feeders can be a great way to attract hummingbirds to our yards, especially if there are not many native flowers in our area. However, it is important to use the correct type of nectar and to clean the feeders regularly to prevent the spread of disease.

Support conservation organizations: There are a number of organizations that are working to protect hummingbirds. By donating to these organizations, we can help them to continue their important work.

Educate others about hummingbirds: By educating others about the threats facing hummingbirds, we can help to raise awareness and encourage people to take action to protect them.
